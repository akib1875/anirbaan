Exoplanets that we know!


Kepler-186f

Kepler-186f was the first rocky planet to be found within the habitable zone -- the region around the host star where the temperature is right for liquid water. This planet is also very close in size to Earth. Even though we may not find out what's going on at the surface of this planet anytime soon, it's a strong reminder of why new technologies are being developed that will enable scientists to get a closer look at distant worlds. Credits: NASA Ames/SETI Institute/JPL-Caltech

Osiris

The first planet to be seen in transit (crossing its star) and the first planet to have it light directly detected. The HD 209458 b transit discovery showed that transit observations were feasible and opened up an entire new realm of exoplanet characterization. Credits: NASA, European Space Agency, Alfred Vidal-Madjar (Institut d'Astrophysique de Paris, CNRS)

55 Cancri e

55 Cancri e is a toasty world that rushes around its star every 18 hours. It orbits so closely -- about 25 times closer than Mercury is to our sun -- that it is tidally locked with one face forever blisters under the heat of its sun. The planet is proposed to have a rocky core surrounded by a layer of water in a "supercritical" state, where it is both liquid and gas, and then the whole planet is thought to be topped by a blanket of steam. Credits: NASA/JPL-Caltech

51 Pegasi b

This giant planet, which is about half the mass of Jupiter and orbits its star every four days, was the first confirmed exoplanet around a sun-like star, a discovery that launched a whole new field of exploration. Credits: NASA/JPL-Caltech

Kepler-10b

Kepler's first rocky planet discovery is a scorched, Earth-size world that scientists believe may have a lava ocean on its surface. Credits: NASA/Kepler Mission/Dana Berry

Kepler-11 system

This was the first compact solar system discovered by Kepler, and it revealed that a system can be tightly packed, with at least five planets within the orbit of Mercury, and still be stable. It touched off a whole new look into planet formation ideas and suggested that multiple small planet systems, like ours, may be common. Credits: NASA/JPL-Caltech

Kepler-16b

A real-life "Tatooine," this planet was Kepler's first discovery of a planet that orbits two stars -- what is known as a circumbinary planet. Credits: NASA/JPL-Caltech

CoRoT 7b

The first super-Earth identified as a rocky exoplanet, this planet proved that worlds like the Earth were indeed possible and that the search for potentially habitable worlds (rocky planets in the habitable zone) might be fruitful. Credits: ESO/L. Cal√ßada

Kepler-22b

A planet in the habitable zone and a possible water-world planet unlike any seen in our solar system. Credits: NASA/Ames/JPL-Caltech

Kepler-444 system

The oldest known planetary system has five terrestrial-sized planets, all in orbital resonance. This weird group showed that solar systems have formed and lived in our galaxy for nearly its entire existence. Credits: Tiago Campante/Peter Devine

Learn More About Exoplanets


Hot Jupiters: These are massive gas giants that orbit very close to their parent stars, resulting in extremely high temperatures. An example is WASP-39b

Super-Earths: These are rocky planets that are more massive than Earth but less massive than Neptune. Kepler-452b is an example, sometimes referred to as an "Earth cousin."

Similar to Uranus and Neptune in our solar system, these planets are composed mainly of ices and gases. An example is OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb.

Ice Giants: Similar to Uranus and Neptune in our solar system, these planets are composed mainly of ices and gases. An example is OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb.

Exoplanets in the Habitable Zone: These planets orbit their stars at distances where liquid water could potentially exist on their surfaces, making them candidates for the possibility of life. TRAPPIST-1e and Kepler-186f are examples.

Rogue Planets: These are planets not bound to any star and instead drift through space. PSO J318.5-22 is an example.

Pulsar Planets: Some planets orbit pulsars (rotating neutron stars). An example is PSR B1257+12 A, B, and C.

Tatooine-like Planets: These are exoplanets in binary star systems, like the fictional planet Tatooine from Star Wars. Kepler-16b is a famous example.

Exoplanets with Unusual Characteristics: Some exoplanets have unique features, such as HD 189733b, known for its blue color due to silicate particles in its atmosphere, or KELT-9b, one of the hottest known exoplanets.

Mini-Neptunes: These are gaseous planets with sizes between Earth and Neptune. GJ 1214b is an example.

Exoplanets with Extreme Conditions: There are exoplanets with extreme temperatures, gravity, or atmospheric conditions. HD 209458b, for instance, is known for its evaporating atmosphere.
